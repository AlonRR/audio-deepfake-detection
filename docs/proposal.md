## Overview

This project covers **both halves** required by the brief for the *Audio deep fakes*
topic: **(A) creation** — cloning my own voice from a few minutes of audio and
synthesizing new speech — and **(B) recognition** — building a detector that decides
whether a clip is real (bona fide) or synthetic (spoof). The two halves are deliberately
linked: the fakes I create in Part A become an *unseen test set* for the detector in
Part B, which lets me probe a real, currently-open research question — *do detectors
trained on today's benchmarks catch fakes from the newest text-to-speech systems?*

Every modelling choice is deliberately anchored to material taught in the course
(RNN/LSTM/GRU, attention/Transformers, CNNs, word/sequence embeddings, GANs, VAEs), so
each decision is defensible in the oral exam.

> **What "show the process" means here.** Per the brief, this is explicitly *not* a
> one-shot implementation. For each half I start from a weak baseline, show where it
> fails, analyse *why*, and iterate — logging learning curves and a hyper-parameter
> search at each step. Two of the models (the Keras TTS baseline; the first-try CNN
> detector) exist **precisely to fail** and motivate the stronger approach.

## Problem & motivation

Neural text-to-speech has crossed the line where a **few minutes of a person's speech**
is enough to clone their voice convincingly (XTTS, VALL-E, OpenVoice, …). This is
useful (accessibility, dubbing) and dangerous (fraud, disinformation, non-consensual
impersonation). The defensive response is **synthetic-speech detection** (a.k.a.
anti-spoofing / audio deepfake detection), the subject of the ASVspoof challenge series.

**Why now / why interesting:** the attack and defence are locked in an arms race, and
the literature explicitly flags a gap — public benchmarks (ASVspoof 2021, In-the-Wild,
Deepfake-Eval-2024) contain **almost no deepfakes generated by the latest 2024–2025 TTS
architectures**. Because I *build* a modern cloner in Part A, I can generate exactly
those out-of-distribution fakes and measure whether a competitive detector generalizes
to them. That closes the loop and gives the project an original, testable contribution
rather than a pure reproduction.

**Concrete goals**
1. Clone my voice from **1–5 min** and synthesize **≥10 clips** of varying length and
   complexity (short commands → long, punctuation-heavy paragraphs).
2. Quantify clone quality with the metrics the brief suggests (it says *"metrics such
   as"*, so these are examples rather than a fixed list): **MOS**, **spectrogram
   similarity**, **speaker-embedding cosine similarity**.
3. Implement a **2020-onward** detector, reproduce a credible **EER** on ASVspoof 2019
   LA, and compare against published numbers.
4. **Cross-test:** run the detector on my own clips and report the generalization gap.

## Background / related work

*(Full annotated list with links in [`literature.md`](literature.md); highlights here.)*

**Voice cloning / TTS.** Classic neural TTS is a **sequence-to-sequence model with
attention** — Tacotron 2 (encoder → attention → autoregressive mel-spectrogram decoder)
plus a neural vocoder. This maps directly onto the course's RNN/LSTM material
(`ML4.pptx`) and the attention lecture (`attention.pptx`); it is essentially the
`exs/ex5` GRU sequence model with an attention bridge and an audio target. Modern
zero-shot cloners replace the RNN with a **GPT-style autoregressive Transformer over
discrete audio tokens** and a **GAN vocoder** (HiFi-GAN): **XTTS-v2** and **YourTTS**
are the reference systems; **VITS** frames the whole pipeline as a conditional **VAE**
with adversarial training — i.e. the exact VAE (`L16_autoencoder`) + GAN (`L18_gan`)
combination taught in the course.

**Anti-spoofing / detection.** Early ASVspoof systems were CNNs on hand-crafted spectral
features (LFCC-LCNN) or end-to-end raw-waveform networks (**RawNet2**, 2021: a fixed
SincNet front-end → residual CNN blocks → GRU → classifier — CNN + GRU, both course
topics). The current state of the art replaces the front-end with a **frozen
self-supervised speech Transformer** (wav2vec2-XLS-R, WavLM) and trains a light back-end
(**AASIST** graph-attention, or an attentive-pooling classifier); this reaches EER as
low as ~0.2% on ASVspoof 2019 LA. The newest systems (**AASIST3**, **XLS-R+SLS**, 2024)
extend this for the harder, crowdsourced **ASVspoof 5** data. The recurring lesson —
*a powerful pretrained representation + a small trainable head beats a big model trained
from scratch* — is the same transfer-learning idea as using pretrained word embeddings
(`word embedding.pptx`).

**Where my work sits.** I reproduce the SSL-frontend recipe (frozen encoder + my own
Keras back-end), benchmark it the standard way (EER / min t-DCF on ASVspoof 2019 LA),
and then add the **cross-generator generalization test** on my own 2025-era fakes — the
documented gap above.

## Approach / method

### Part A — Creation (two systems, on purpose)

**A1. Baseline — a Tacotron2-style TTS I build in Keras/TensorFlow.**
Character/phoneme embedding → BiLSTM encoder → **location-sensitive attention** →
autoregressive LSTM decoder emitting mel frames → Griffin-Lim (and, if time allows, a
small neural vocoder) to waveform. Trained only on my 1–5 min.
*Course anchors:* `ML4.pptx` (RNN/LSTM), `attention.pptx` (attention), `exs/ex5`
(Embedding→GRU→Dense with training-curve logging) as the starting scaffold.
*Expected outcome:* intelligible-ish but rough and unstable — **this is the point.** I
analyse the failure modes (too little data, attention alignment collapse, exposure bias,
Griffin-Lim artifacts) and use them to justify moving to a pretrained model.

**A2. Main — fine-tune XTTS-v2 on the same 1–5 min (PyTorch container).**
Start from zero-shot cloning (reference-encoder only), then fine-tune on my speaker and
compare. Show the **fine-tuning loss curve** and a zero-shot-vs-fine-tuned quality
comparison.
*Course anchors:* GPT-style Transformer decoder (`attention.pptx`), speaker embedding
(`word embedding.pptx`), HiFi-GAN vocoder (`L18_gan`).

**Synthesis set.** From both systems, generate **≥10 clips** spanning short phrases,
medium sentences, and long punctuation-heavy paragraphs (varying "length and
complexity", per the brief) → `data/generated/`.

### Evaluation of creation (the metrics the brief suggests)

| Metric | How | Compares |
|---|---|---|
| **MOS** (naturalness, 1–5) | small human listening panel via a form; ≥10 raters where possible | real vs A1 vs A2 |
| **Spectrogram similarity** | **MCD** (mel-cepstral distortion) + log-mel L2/SSIM (`librosa`) | cloned vs real reference |
| **Speaker-embedding cosine** | **ECAPA-TDNN** (SpeechBrain) embeddings, cosine similarity | cloned vs real speaker |

*(Optional, clearly flagged as outside course materials: an automatic MOS predictor
— UTMOS/NISQA — as a cheap proxy to sanity-check the human MOS.)*

### Part B — Detection (SSL frontend + my own Keras back-end)

Built as a **process**, three escalating attempts:

1. **First try (Keras CNN):** log-mel / LFCC spectrogram → small CNN (dropout + BatchNorm,
   in the style of `exs/ex4`). Establishes the pipeline and a weak EER baseline.
2. **Published baseline (pure Keras):** re-implement **RawNet2** (or LFCC-LCNN) so I have
   an official system with **published EER to compare against** (satisfies "compare with
   others' results").
3. **Main system:** freeze a **wav2vec2-XLS-R / WavLM** encoder (PyTorch container), use
   it as a **fixed feature extractor**, cache embeddings to `/models`/`/projects`, then
   **build and train the back-end in Keras** (attentive statistics pooling, optionally a
   light AASIST-style graph-attention module) in the TensorFlow container. Because
   features are cached to disk, the two frameworks never share an environment.

*Course anchors:* CNNs (`ML3.pptx`), GRU (`ML4.pptx`), attention (`attention.pptx`), and
transfer learning via a frozen pretrained representation (analogous to pretrained
embeddings, `word embedding.pptx`).

**Multiple tries / hyper-parameter search (required):** for the main system I sweep
learning rate, which SSL layer(s) to pool, pooling type (mean vs attentive), back-end
width/depth, and augmentation (RawBoost-style). I log **learning curves** for each run,
keep **at least one deliberately failed configuration**, analyse it, and defend the final
choice.

### Innovation — the cross-generator test (links A and B)

Run the fully-trained detector on my **own generated clips** from A1 and A2 — a
synthesizer it never saw in training — and report the EER gap versus ASVspoof. This
directly probes the documented "no latest-TTS in benchmarks" gap and is the project's
own contribution. Stretch: fine-tune / augment the detector with a handful of my fakes
and measure the recovery.

## Data

- **Target speaker (creation):** **my own voice**, ~1–5 min of clean read speech (quiet
  room, single mic, 16–24 kHz). Clean rights and consent by construction. A short
  held-out reference set is kept for the spectrogram/embedding metrics.

  train/dev/eval split; published EER/t-DCF to compare against). *First check whether it
  is already staged on the lab's `/datasets` (`/shenkar-data/fg/datasets`) NFS share
  before downloading.*
- **Generalization (optional):** **In-the-Wild** (Müller 2022) for a second
  out-of-distribution point, plus **my own clips** as the primary novel test.
- **Preprocessing:** resample to the model's rate, trim silence, 16-bit PCM wav; features
  = log-mel / LFCC (baselines) and cached SSL embeddings (main). Licensing: ASVspoof is
  research-licensed; my voice is self-recorded.

## Experiments & evaluation

**Creation:** table of real vs A1 vs A2 on MOS, MCD, and speaker cosine; spectrogram
figures; the XTTS fine-tuning curve; a written failure analysis of A1.

**Detection:** EER + **min t-DCF** + **DET curve** on ASVspoof 2019 LA for all three
attempts (first-try CNN → RawNet2 → SSL+Keras), against published baselines; the
hyper-parameter sweep table + learning curves; the failed-run analysis.

**Cross-test:** EER of the final detector on In-the-Wild and on my A1/A2 clips; the
generalization gap and its interpretation.

**Success criteria:** (1) A2 clearly beats A1 on all three creation metrics and is
recognizably my voice; (2) the SSL detector reproduces a competitive ASVspoof 2019 LA EER
(target low single digits) and beats the two baselines; (3) the cross-test yields a clear,
well-analysed generalization result (a gap is a *finding*, not a failure).

**Hardware:** Shenkar Virtual Lab — **NVIDIA L4, 24 GB, CUDA 13.0**, via WireGuard VPN →
JupyterHub (`/jupyter/hub/login`) with **Slurm** (`sbatch --gres=gpu:1`) and two ready

(`tensorflow-25.02.sif`). NFS: `/datasets`, `/projects/<course>/`, `/models/$(whoami)`.

**Fits the L4 (24 GB):** XTTS-v2 fine-tune ✅; frozen SSL feature extraction ✅ (cheap);
SSL fine-tune (≈300M, small batch / LoRA) ✅ with care; RawNet2 / CNN / back-end training
✅ easily. No billion-parameter from-scratch training is needed.

**Rough training budget:** XTTS fine-tune ~hours; SSL feature extraction over ASVspoof LA
~1–2 h (one pass, cached); each back-end run minutes–~1 h → many sweep runs feasible in
the 3-week window.

**Risks & fallback (feasibility-first):**
- *SSL back-end underperforms or stalls* → fall back to the pure-Keras **RawNet2** as the
  headline detector (still 2021, still competitive).
- *XTTS fine-tune is finicky* → use **zero-shot XTTS** as A2 and keep the zero-shot-vs-…
  comparison; the A1-vs-A2 story survives.
- *Time pressure* → **drop the Keras TTS baseline (A1)** and compare zero-shot vs
  fine-tuned XTTS instead; detection half is unaffected.
- *MOS panel too small* → report the auto-MOS proxy alongside a smaller human panel.
